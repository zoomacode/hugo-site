The last year, maybe year and a half, I was thinking about that one thought that the Hayao Miyazaki movies are sort of a manifestation or a love letter to regular women who demonstrate strength not through exercising power or physical abilities, but by drawing it from love and care for the people they care the most or things they care the most for.

For example, in Nausicaa, we see how a little girl, believing in the friendly intent of nature, stands between her people and seemingly hostile nature, establishing peace through sacrificing herself for the things that she loves the most. Similarly, in Spirited Away, Chihiro takes care of her family and her friend, starting as a person who doesn't care much about anything but herself. She is a child, after all. She is moving from one city to another city, new school and so on. Why would you expect any different? But then she grows through hard labor and a bunch of obstacles. She demonstrates care for people you wouldn't expect her to care about, like that girl she met in the bathhouse who became her friend, Sen, who also became her friend, then even your bubby and Niva became her friend in the end. They were all a little bit hostile towards her, and still that hugging desire to help save the day.

Similarly, in the Wind Rises, Sophia takes care of Hal, who, in the beginning, appears to be like a child in an adult body. Later we discover that it's not the full story, but again, for seemingly simple things, just taking care of the house or showing small acts of care, for the whole movie she changed the course of history. For a very long time, it was the main Oh, yes.

In Totoro, we have this similar picture where a little girl is holding the house together in a family pulled apart: her mother gets sick, moving into a different place, finding new friends and so on. For a long time, those movies were sort of a way to express the gratitude for how much regular women are holding the world together behind the curtain, stay in the shadows, etc. etc. It was resonating with me a lot. I kind of feel the same gratitude towards my wife, for example, or my mother, or just women in general who stay home and do a lot. That's something they do; usually not an epic story you would hear as a legend. It's not like Iliad and Odyssey sort of stuff; you don't hear much about that routine, everyday work your way type of actions. It was the main narrative about the main understanding of Hayao Miyazaki's art for the last year.

Yeah, then I started wondering this. It's kind of an unusual topic in the world, where most of the stories, at least the populace, if you look at the movies, if you look at that heroic narrative of Bushido and samurai, built around ultimate self-sacrifice and annihilation in the supernova explosion kind of style, and Miyazaki stories with little women in the center of the story, are kinda like an attempt to show what is behind the curtain, what is in the shadow, what is that love that feeds all those people who have the luxury to sacrifice themselves and be a part of a bigger story. Those dragon slayers still need to eat, right? It's not like they're magically finding food out of nowhere. Yes, they can hunt, but besides hunting, what is heroic about fighting? I assume maybe it's our own words. There is actually quite something in fighting for something, even if nothing backs you up.

Anyway, when we are saying we are fighting for our country, our family, that country and family, those females are actually providing quite a bit to those heroes. Hayao Miyazaki is bringing it up to the light and telling the story of little things changing the world, not necessarily changing but maintaining one little piece at a time.

I realized that female characters were usually not alone in his stories. It's just that the stories are told through the stories I selected or love the most are told through the lens of male characters as well, and they do their part. They maintain the world in a different way, for the craftsmanship, for farming, through fighting their own little fights. They are not like a warrior per se who would go and kill, but they are, in a way, maintaining order and providing for their families and shielding them from something harmful.

If you think about it a little bit more, it's just a story about regular life, about men and women. Just the stories I selected are more from the land perspective of the women, which are a story in Hayao Miyazaki's portfolio, which are told from the standpoint of little man as well.

And if you remember, for a very, very long time, almost the whole existence, we lived in that state where everyone suffered a lot. Marriage was partnership in a way; everyone was doing their own part. Someone stayed home and was cooking and doing some other stuff; someone went to the field and was doing some other stuff, like hard labor, or some of those go hunting and may never get back. The child mortality was brutal, so it's real stories about regular people, regular life, the heroism of persistence, about happiness in supporting each other. That's why they may resonate on that deeper level, where the fairy tale at the surface somehow makes you feel a part of the story, somehow resonates with you.

I'm just writing it to you, just to share some thoughts about it and express some gratitude for both hard-working men and women, and gratitude for my wife, who is taking care of our home and staying beside me for, god knows how long, like 20 years, 21. There is no conclusion; it is just some soul spilled over a sheet of paper.
